## Full Bio (for dedicated About/Author page, ~500 words)

### About Sarah Mitchell

I didn't come to yoga retreats through enlightenment. I came through exhaustion.

For eleven years I worked in project management at a mid-sized investment firm in London. I was good at the job. I was also, by 2017, running entirely on caffeine, cortisol, and the particular kind of pride that comes from never admitting you're struggling.

In March 2018 I had a panic attack in a meeting. Not a subtle one. The kind where your colleague asks if you need a glass of water and you genuinely cannot answer. I took two weeks off. My doctor suggested rest. My therapist suggested boundaries. My friend Nina — a yoga teacher I'd always half-dismissed as "one of those people" — suggested I book a retreat before I talked myself out of it.

I booked seven days in Rishikesh for £380. I told myself it was just a holiday.

It wasn't just a holiday.

**What happened after Rishikesh**

I came back different enough that people at work noticed. Not floaty-different. Quieter-different. More able to pause before reacting. I slept properly for the first time in two years.

I also came back obsessed with the question of *why* that particular retreat worked when so many others I'd subsequently hear about from friends had felt like expensive disappointments. What was the difference? Was it the teacher? The structure? The location? The group size? The absence of wifi?

I spent the next five years finding out.

**The 14 retreats (and what they taught me)**

I've now attended 14 retreats across 9 countries. Here's the honest version:

- **Rishikesh, India** — 7 days, $380. Vinyasa and meditation. Basic accommodation, extraordinary teachers. My benchmark for value.
- **Ubud, Bali** — 10 days, $1,200. Yoga, sound healing, rice field walks. Where I learned that "Instagram-perfect" and "genuinely nourishing" are not the same thing.
- **Algarve, Portugal** — 5 days, $1,800. Couples retreat with my then-partner. Beautifully run. Useful for relationships that are *almost* working, not ones that are clearly broken.
- **Oaxaca, Mexico** — 8 days, $950. Yin yoga and cacao ceremonies. Underrated destination. Incredible food.
- **Atlas Mountains, Morocco** — 6 days, $1,100. Women-only. The most emotionally intense retreat I've attended. Not for everyone. Absolutely right for some.
- **Tuscany, Italy** — 7 days, $4,200. Luxury immersion. Exceptional on every metric. Worth it *if* that price point genuinely doesn't create financial stress — otherwise the anxiety undoes the yoga.
- **Tulum, Mexico** — 5 days, $1,600. Surf and yoga. The best sleep of my life. Also where I learned to spot "retreat theatre" — programming designed to photograph well rather than serve the participant.
- **Colorado Rockies, USA** — 4 days, $1,350. Altitude yoga and hiking. A gateway retreat for sceptics. I've recommended it to three corporate friends and all three came back converts.
- **Sedona, Arizona** — 6 days, $2,100. Vortex meditation and hot yoga. Genuinely surprising. I went in cynical about the energy-vortex framing and left not entirely cynical.
- **Kerala, India** — 10 days, $780. Ayurvedic and pranayama focus. The most physically transformative. Also the hardest.
- **Koh Samui, Thailand** — 7 days, $1,900. Detox and yoga. I have complicated feelings. Review forthcoming.
- **Sintra, Portugal** — 4 days, $890. Forest bathing and restorative yoga. Short and underestimated. Excellent for retreat beginners.
- **Puerto Vallarta, Mexico** — 6 days, $1,450. Mixed-level vinyasa by the ocean. The teacher was exceptional. The organisation was chaotic. Both things can be true.
- **Fuerteventura, Canary Islands** — 5 days, $1,050. Surf and yoga. Cold mornings, warm evenings, and the best value I've found in Europe.

**Why I built this site**

I got tired of reading retreat reviews written by people who'd attended one retreat, or by writers who'd been comped a stay in exchange for a glowing writeup, or by platforms that rank retreats based on who pays them the most affiliate commission.

I have no financial relationship with the retreats I review. If I recommend something here, it's because I'd tell my best friend to book it. If I don't recommend something, I'll tell you exactly why — not in euphemisms, but plainly.

I'm not a yoga teacher. I don't have a 500-hour certification. What I have is 14 retreats attended at my own expense, a background in evaluating complex projects, and a genuine inability to write something I don't believe.

That's the whole deal.
