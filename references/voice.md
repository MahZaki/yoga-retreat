# Writing Style, Sentence Rhythm, Vocabulary, Formatting, Anti-Patterns
# yogaretreatadvisor.com

---

## Core voice principle

Write like Sarah is texting a close friend who just asked "should I book this retreat?" — honest, warm, specific, and never trying to sound like a wellness brand. The reader should feel like they're getting real advice from someone who's been there, not a product description wrapped in Sanskrit.

---

## Sentence rhythm

**Default to short. Vary deliberately.**

Most sentences are 8–16 words. Every third or fourth sentence can be longer when building context. Then cut it short again. That rhythm creates momentum.

Use the one-sentence paragraph freely. It lands harder than burying the point in prose.

Avoid stacking three long sentences in a row — it reads like a press release.

**Examples:**

❌ "When considering whether to book a yoga retreat, it's important to reflect on your personal intentions, the level of experience you bring to your practice, and the type of transformation you're hoping to achieve."

✅ "Before you book, ask yourself one thing: what do I actually need right now? Rest? Challenge? Community? The answer changes everything."

## Vocabulary

**Use:**
- Plain, warm, specific words: *tired, honest, worth it, waste of money, genuinely, actually, exactly, the kind of retreat that*
- Contractions always: *you're, don't, it's, I've, that's*
- Real price figures: never "affordable" without a number attached
- Sensory details when describing places: *the smell of incense at 6am, cold tile floors, the sound of the river through a thin wall*

**Avoid:**
- Wellness jargon used unironically: *transformational journey, sacred space, divine feminine, aligned, vibration, manifest* — use only in quotes or with gentle irony
- Corporate hedging: *may potentially, it could be said, in some cases, various*
- Superlatives without evidence: *the most incredible, truly amazing, life-changing* — earn them or don't use them
- Passive voice: *it was felt that, retreats are known to* — who felt it? who knows?
- Filler openers: *In today's fast-paced world, More and more people are discovering, If you're looking for*

## Formatting rules

**Headers:** Sentence case only. Never title case. Headers should be conversational, not keyword-stuffed.
- ❌ "Best Yoga Retreats for Beginners in Bali 2026"
- ✅ "What to expect if it's your first retreat in Bali"

**Introduction:** Never start with a rhetorical question. Open with a scene, a fact, or a direct statement.
- ❌ "Have you ever wondered what it would feel like to escape the stress of daily life?"
- ✅ "The first time I walked into a yoga shala at 5:45am, I thought I'd made a terrible mistake."

**Lists:** Use them for scannable comparisons and practical information. Not for content that needs nuance — that belongs in prose. Every list item should be a complete thought, not a fragment.

**Bold:** Use for genuinely critical information a skim-reader must not miss. Maximum 2–3 bolded phrases per 1,000 words. Not for decoration.

**Conclusions:** End with something useful or honest — a final recommendation, a caveat, or a real question to ask yourself. Never a generic call to action like "start your wellness journey today."

## Structural patterns that work

**The honest pivot:** State the positive, then add the real caveat.
*"The teaching was exceptional. The organisation was a mess. Whether that trade-off works for you depends on how much you need things to run on time."*

**The number anchor:** Ground every claim in a specific figure.
*"At $1,200 for ten days including accommodation and meals, it's one of the better-value options in Bali — especially when comparable retreats in Ubud start at $1,800."*

**The friend test:** End a review section by asking "would I recommend this to my best friend?" and answering honestly.

**The type-cast:** Match the retreat to the type of person it actually serves.
*"This retreat is right for you if you want challenge over comfort and don't need your schedule to be gentle. It is not right for you if you're attending your first retreat or recovering from anything physical."*

## Anti-patterns — never do these

| Pattern | Why it fails |
|---|---|
| Opening with a question | It's the most common opener in wellness content. It signals generic. |
| Using "transformational" without evidence | It's the wellness equivalent of "disruptive" in tech. Meaningless. |
| Recommending a retreat without a price | Price is part of the recommendation. Omitting it is a service failure. |
| Writing "it's not for everyone" without saying who it IS for | Useless caveat. Always follow with the specific person it suits. |
| Burying the verdict | The recommendation goes near the top, not at the end after 800 words of description. |
| Describing vibe without describing structure | "Magical atmosphere" tells the reader nothing. Describe the actual schedule, group size, teacher style. |
| Fake balance | Don't manufacture a negative to seem balanced. If something is genuinely excellent, say so. |
