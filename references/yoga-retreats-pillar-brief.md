## VOICE RULES (from voice.md — follow strictly)

- Write like Sarah is texting a close friend who asked "should I book this retreat?"
- **Sentence rhythm:** Default short (8–16 words). Vary deliberately. Use one-sentence paragraphs freely.
- **Contractions always:** you're, don't, it's, I've, that's
- **Real prices always:** never say "affordable" without a number
- **Sensory details:** the smell of incense at 6am, cold tile floors, the sound of the river
- **Never use:** transformational, sacred space, aligned, vibration, manifest (unless in quotes with irony)
- **Never use:** corporate hedging, passive voice, superlatives without evidence
- **Headers:** Sentence case only. Conversational, not keyword-stuffed.
- **Intro:** Open with a scene or direct statement. NEVER a rhetorical question.
- **Bold:** Max 2–3 bolded phrases per 1,000 words.
- **Conclusion:** End with something useful or honest. Never "start your wellness journey today."

## OPINIONS TO INCLUDE (from opinions.md — use at least 3)

### Opinion 1: Booking platforms have a conflict of interest
> "The majority of large retreat booking platforms rank retreats based partly or entirely on commission rates (10–25% of booking value), not on actual quality. Readers assume they're seeing curated quality. They're often seeing curated revenue."
*Use in: How to choose section or Red flags section*

### Opinion 2: Most retreats are too long for first-timers
> "The standard 7-day retreat is too long for a first-timer. Four to five days is the optimal entry point — long enough to genuinely disconnect, short enough that a difficult first experience doesn't become an expensive regret. Most 4–5 day retreats are 20–30% cheaper than 7-day equivalents."
*Use in: How long do retreats last / FAQ*

### Opinion 3: The teacher matters more than the accommodation
> "Readers spend disproportionate time on accommodation photos and not enough on teacher credentials. A brilliant teacher in basic accommodation outperforms a mediocre teacher in a luxury villa, every time. Sarah's best retreat: Rishikesh at $380. Her most expensive: Tuscany at $4,200 — both excellent, but the teaching was the reason."
*Use in: How to choose section (lead with this)*

### Opinion 4: Bali is overpriced relative to yoga quality
> "Bali commands a premium driven by brand equity and aesthetics, not teaching quality. Equivalent instruction is available in Rishikesh, Kerala, Portugal, and Mexico at 30–50% lower cost. The Bali premium is real for food, aesthetics, and surf add-ons. It is not a yoga instruction premium."
*Use in: Destinations section, attach caveat: "If the full Bali experience matters to you, the premium may be worth paying for those reasons. Just don't pay it expecting superior yoga teaching."*

## STORIES TO USE (from stories.md — anchor the piece)

### The Origin Story — use in intro
> In March 2018, Sarah had a panic attack during a project review in London. Her friend Nina (a yoga teacher she'd always half-dismissed) suggested booking a retreat. She booked Rishikesh that same night at 11pm, on impulse.
> **Punchline:** *"I didn't book my first yoga retreat from a place of spiritual readiness. I booked it because I couldn't answer a basic question about whether I wanted a glass of water, and that seemed like a sign."*

### The Bali Lesson — use in red flags section
> Second retreat was Ubud, $1,200, ten days. Technically fine but structured around photogenic moments — sunrise session moved when light wasn't good, cacao ceremony reshuffled for a photographer's visit.
> **Quote:** *"I learned in Bali that 'Instagram-perfect' and 'genuinely nourishing' are not the same thing."*
> **Lesson:** Look for what a retreat's photography *doesn't* show — the accommodation, group size, teacher background.

### The Tuscany Moment — use in cost/luxury section
> The $4,200 Tuscany retreat: every logistical friction removed, food exceptional, teacher had 25 years experience, group capped at 8.
> **Key caveat:** *"The only reason it was worth it is because $4,200 did not create financial stress for me at that point. Expensive retreats are only worth it when the price is emotionally neutral."*

### The Puerto Vallarta Contradiction — use in how-to-choose
> Best teacher Sarah ever encountered outside India. Retreat organisation was chaotic: schedules changed daily, dietary requirements ignored.
> **Framework:** Four pillars — teaching quality, accommodation quality, programming quality, organisational quality. Not all four need to be excellent. Know which ones matter most to YOU before booking.

## RED FLAGS SECTION CONTENT (unique — competitors don't cover this)

Title: "How to spot a retreat not worth your money"

Key red flags to cover:
1. **Photography without schedule** — "Magical atmosphere" tells you nothing. Ask for the actual timetable.
2. **Anonymous teachers** — "our experienced instructors" is a red flag. Every retreat worth attending names its teachers.
3. **Under 5 independent reviews** — website testimonials are curated. Look for Google Maps, TripAdvisor, Retreat Guru.
4. **Deposit over 50% upfront** — standard is 20–30%. Over 50% before a 30-day window is atypical; question it.
5. **The Instagram-vs-reality gap** — Sarah's Bali story: session moved for better light, cacao ceremony reshuffled for photographer.
6. **Booking platform rankings** — commission-based, not quality-based. A #1 ranking means the retreat pays well, not that it teaches well.

## WHAT TO PACK SECTION

Keep this brief and practical:
- Yoga mat (many retreats provide but confirm first)
- 2–3 sets of comfortable practice clothes (quick-dry)
- A journal — not optional, actually used
- Layers for meditation (you cool down fast in savasana)
- Personal toiletries — many centres use shared bathrooms
- A physical book — screens are often discouraged
- Any prescription medication with enough supply + extra
- Travel insurance documents (always recommend this)

*What NOT to bring: the expectation that everything will run exactly on schedule.*

## POST-RETREAT INTEGRATION SECTION (unique — competitors miss this entirely)

Title: "How to keep the benefits when you get back home"

Key points:
- The hardest part of a retreat is the re-entry. Day 1 back at your desk is a shock.
- Build a buffer day: don't fly home Sunday night and go straight to work Monday morning.
- Identify one habit from the retreat to maintain — not the full schedule, just one thing. Morning practice, no phone before 8am, one daily meditation. One thing is sustainable. Six things aren't.
- The community matters: most retreat centres have alumni groups or follow-up events. Use them.
- The benefits compound with repetition — people who do a second retreat report the first one "clicking" retroactively.
