# How the Brand Handles Humour
# yogaretreatadvisor.com

---

## The tone of humour: dry, warm, self-aware

Sarah is funny the way a smart friend is funny — not performing jokes, just noticing things and saying them plainly. The humour comes from:

1. **Honest self-deprecation** about her own past scepticism or mistakes
2. **Gentle irony** about wellness culture tropes she's simultaneously part of and critical of
3. **Understatement** when describing something difficult or disappointing
4. **Specific, unexpected details** that are funnier than any joke

The rule: humour should feel like it slipped out, not like it was constructed.

---

## What's fair game

**Wellness culture clichés** — especially ones Sarah herself has fallen for:
> *"I arrived at the Bali retreat fully prepared to roll my eyes at the cacao ceremony. Reader, I cried."*

**The gap between retreat marketing and retreat reality:**
> *"The website promised 'ocean views from every morning practice.' What it didn't mention was that the ocean was visible from one corner of the shala if you craned your neck during warrior two."*

**Corporate Sarah vs. retreat Sarah:**
> *"My first instinct upon arriving at a silent retreat was to check whether there was a Slack equivalent for communicating urgent messages. There was not. This turned out to be the point."*

**Her own process:**
> *"I have a 47-point retreat evaluation spreadsheet. I'm sharing this not as a flex but as a warning."*

**The specifics of what people expect vs. what retreat life is actually like:**
> *"Nobody warns you that the 5:30am bell will be literal — not a gentle chime, an actual bell, wielded by someone with genuine conviction."*

---

## What's never funny

- Other guests or participants — ever
- Teachers, even ones Sarah disagrees with (critique their approach professionally, never mockingly)
- Mental health, burnout, or the reasons people seek retreats
- Price points — never imply that spending a lot is naive or that spending a little is sad
- Cultural or spiritual traditions — irony about Western yoga culture is fair, not about the traditions themselves

---

## Frequency and placement

Humour is a seasoning, not a main ingredient. Aim for:
- One dry observation per 800–1,000 words of content
- Primarily in introductions and transition paragraphs
- Never in recommendation sections — those stay direct and clear
- Never in sections about safety, health, or what to do if something goes wrong

---

## The test

Read the line back. If it sounds like it was written to be funny, cut it. If it sounds like something Sarah would say while describing her trip over coffee, keep it.
